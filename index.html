What do we check?
Signals read straight from your site's public files: robots.txt (crawl rules), llms.txt (a proposed standard for guiding LLMs to your key content), sitemap.xml (page discovery), whether your robots.txt explicitly addresses known AI crawlers like GPTBot, ClaudeBot, or CCBot, whether your site is agent-accessible (markdown content negotiation, JSON-LD, schema.org markup), and whether your site advertises support for emerging agent protocols — WebMCP, MCP, UCP, ACP, and AP2.
What is Content Accessibility (Markdown, JSON-LD, Schema.org)?
These checks look at how easily an agent can extract meaning from your pages, not just crawl them: Markdown negotiation checks whether your site returns a clean Markdown response when requested with Accept: text/markdown, saving agents from parsing HTML noise (this check is best-effort — the public CORS proxy may not forward the header, so a "not found" isn't conclusive); JSON-LD checks for <script type="application/ld+json"> blocks on your homepage, which give agents machine-readable structured data; Schema.org checks whether that structured data (or your page's microdata) actually uses the schema.org vocabulary, the most widely recognized standard for describing entities on the web.
What are WebMCP, MCP, UCP, ACP, and AP2?
These are emerging "agentic era" protocols that let AI agents interact with your site programmatically instead of just reading pages: MCP (Model Context Protocol) and WebMCP let agents discover and call tools or context your site exposes; UCP, ACP (Agentic Commerce Protocol), and AP2 (Agent Payments Protocol) are emerging standards for agent-driven commerce and payments. We look for a published manifest — e.g. /.well-known/mcp.json — and fall back to scanning your homepage for a reference to each protocol. Since these standards are still young, this check is best-effort: a "not found" result doesn't guarantee a site has no support, only that we found no public signal of it.
